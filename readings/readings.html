On "In Plato's Cave"

Sontag dwells on what the photograph is extensively. Though it can be distorted by changing the subject, leaving information out of frame, or editing the image outright, there seems to be a fundamental truth to photography that is unlike other forms of art, or other forms of record. A camera can only capture what is there, and in so doing, turns the photograph's subject into a literal object. I think my relationship with photography is more informed by this view of objectification than most. My mother is a photographer, and years of seeing her clients pulled up in Lightroom trained my mind to view them not as individuals with their own rich inner world, but as still images, a part of the background of my home. I saw hundreds of faces belonging to people I would never know, yet I was privy to the perverse and intimate moments of editing. I began to see the camera as something unnatural- something about the subtractive nature of photography beginning to unnerve me. Not unlike the written word, I became acutely aware that every photograph left out far more than it included- not only the inner world of the subject, but the momentum of the scene, the details beyond the frame. I never adopted my mother's art form, preferring poetry and prose. With the written word, I had absolute control over my creations, and I found myself hesitant to engage with the visual medium as a whole. So much of it seemed permanent, indelible, and stark, where language allowed changes and fluidity. I remember vividly the rise of Instagram, suddenly all my peers were posting photos of their pets, their dinners, the small mundanities that made up their lives. This behavior is normative now, but at the time it felt alien and exhibitionistic. I never took up the habit of photographing my daily life, either. Selfies became their own language- specific poses, facial expressions, lighting techniques becoming common knowledge. Again, I fell behind my peers, unwilling or unable to engage in this visual language. My refusal to engage in social media began to manifest in a technical illiteracy that drove a small wedge between myself and my peers. Over the past few years, I've renegotiated my relationship with the photograph. I carry a camera in my pocket, like the rest of the world, and I've been known to use it to snap a photo of my cats, or my meals, or a quick selfie. Part of the reason for my enrollment in this course is in the hopes that I'll find a way to connect with the medium as my mother did - as art, rather than consumption. Understanding the generative power the medium holds, I'm daunted but curious to find what I will choose to capture, what story I will choose to tell. Unlike the other art forms I engage in, I am bound by that which surrounds me- forcing me to look at the world through a different lens, in the hopes of seeing that which is worthy of documentation, preservation, and transformation with this new tool.

On AI Slop

This article speaks to a deeply held fear that has been gnawing at me for some time now. Malik says "You can't believe your eyes, but also what can you believe if not your eyes?", and perfectly encapsulates my concern. Humanity has long wondered about the nature of image and reality - as early as Plato, who (in The Sophist) spends much time debating whether an image of a thing is any less real than the thing itself. Art and media may not occupy the same space in reality as the things they depict, but remain deeply real in the way they impact us, on an individual and sociopolitical level. Each piece of art is imbued with intent and meaning, the same way a speech might be, or a manifesto. Art, however, differs fundamentally in terms of its accessibility. Language, education, and access limit the effective spread of text, while an image can be understood by nearly all who see it. Though only a small portion of the world's population have read the Communist Manifesto, millions will recognize the hammer and sickle; and through this may be able to infer the values behind the movement. This is why art has always been a tool for propaganda - its ability to condense a worldview into an image is the perfect vessel for normalization, and to spread a view of the world with plausible deniability regarding its interpretations. Art is a tool to communicate, and is perhaps the most efficient method we have in the age of mass media. AI art takes this established pattern, and alters it. AI slop fundamentally lacks meaning. Even when generated with an intentional prompt, there is no decision making behind the final product. Each instance is merely a recreation of the data AI is trained upon, filtered through the lens of the prompt. It is self-fulfilling, saying nothing, and has nearly entirely drowned out real voices. I'm chronically offline, with next to no social media, and even I am inundated with AI content everywhere I turn - from spotify playlists to entire youtube videos. This new mode, where anyone can create anything, with no true intent behind it, is damaging our ability to connect with the world authentically. When I must doubt every video, every audio clip, every image I come across, must verify authenticity, it leaves me paralyzed. I empathize with those who have become lost in this sea of garbage, even if I cannot quite find it in me to forgive them. Ultimately, scepticism must become our shield against the inundation of falsehoods and slop; but even then, the propaganda machine has done its job. Even this, though, is not a win. The purpose of propaganda in the modern era is not to change minds - but to normalize extremes and exhaust opposition. The mental energy spent on simply deciphering what is reality and what is a conjurer's trick is mental energy not spent on organizing, on aiding the community, on self betterment. The zone is flooded, the genie is out of the bottle, and I fear this may be the new default, without sweeping change and regulations that seem unlikely to come. Malik calls this paralysis and exhaustion "sleepwalking through disaster", and one could hardly come up with a more fitting representation. I just wish that we knew how to wake up.
